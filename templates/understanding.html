The SQ3R method

Ever tried to tackle a textbook chapter on a confusing topic, and gotten so overwhelmed you couldnâ€™t take a single thing in? The SQ3R method might be just what you need. - studytime

Survey. Read the textbook/teacher's notes/example calculation. Question. Write down everything you find confusing. Re-read. Read it over again, and try to answer your questions. Recite. After each chunk of text/major step, try to explain in your own words what just happened. Revise. Write a summary of everything you learned.

The Feynman Technique

For something a bit more lowkey, but equally effective, give the Feynman Technique a go - studytime

Study, however you like. Then teach it back to someone else. This could be anyone, including a wall or your dog. Then study again, focusing on the parts you couldn't explain. Finally, make yourself a summary sheet to remember it. This could be notes, a diagram, or mind map.



Use your words

When taking notes by hand, it's often impossible to write everything down; students have to... pay attention to the information, process, and prioritize it - scientificamerican

Get out a notebook. Write down what you do understand, and what questions you have. Process it.

Additional material

Videos, podcasts, and live streams can each be a valuable way to convey information, if they exist - hubspot

If you can't understand the textbook or your teacher's notes, maybe it's time to stop re-reading it.



Update your notes

Read the text again. See if you can add to your notes now that you have had some time to digest what you have read - learningessentials

Updating your notes is a great way to find gaps in your knowledge, or past mistakes.

Leitner System

The Leitner system is one of the best study techniques based on flashcards - usa.edu

Make your flashcards. Find three or four boxes. All the flashcards start in box one. If you learn a card, move it up a box. If you fail a card, move it down a box. Focus most of your time on the lowest box.
